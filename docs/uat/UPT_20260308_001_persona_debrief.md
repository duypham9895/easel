## Part I -- Linh (Moon) Exit Interview

### Q1: "Does this app actually understand you as a woman? Why or why not?"

Yes -- about 80%. The app understands my *cycle* really well. The phase descriptions matched how I actually felt almost every day. When it said "You may feel more sensitive or irritable" on Day 25 of my cycle, I was literally fighting tears over a cereal commercial. The luteal greeting "Slow down. You don't need to do everything" hit me so hard I screenshot it. The way it talks about my body -- "Co the ban dang lam viec rat vat va" (Your body is working very hard) -- that's not clinical. That's respectful.

But here is where it falls short: it understands my cycle as a *pattern*, not as my *life*. When my period was predicted but hadn't actually started, the app just cheerfully rolled into menstrual phase content like nothing happened. It didn't ask me "Hey, has your period started?" It just assumed. And that moment -- when I was anxious about being late, staring at this app confidently telling me I was on Day 5 of a period I hadn't gotten -- that was the moment I felt the least understood. The app understood my average cycle. It did not understand *me* that day.

Also, the app doesn't know about my life outside my uterus. I'm a graphic designer with deadlines. Some days my mood is low not because of hormones but because a client rejected my work. The check-in lets me log mood and symptoms, but there's no context for "why." The AI insight always attributes my mood to my phase, which sometimes feels reductive. On Day 13 I had a headache from screen fatigue, not from ovulation. The app didn't know the difference.

So does it understand me as a woman? It understands my biology with surprising empathy. It does not yet understand me as a *whole person*.

Yes. Genuinely, measurably yes.

The second example is Day 17. Minh got a notification saying my period was coming in 3 days. He stopped at the store on his way home and bought my favorite snacks -- banh trang tron and a yogurt drink -- without me asking, without me even mentioning I was feeling bad. When I asked how he knew, he just smiled. That kind of proactive care didn't exist in our relationship before this app.

But I should be honest: the Day 18 argument happened despite the app. Minh had the advice "avoid arguments" on his dashboard but didn't check it that evening. The app had the right answer but couldn't deliver it at the right time. So the improvement is real but incomplete. The app helps when Minh checks it. It can't help when he doesn't.

### Q3: "What's the one thing you wish the app did that it doesn't?"

A "Period Started" button on the dashboard.

Right now, to log that my period has arrived, I have to go to Settings, scroll to Cycle Settings, find the date picker for "Last period start date," spin the calendar to today, and save. That is the most important thing I do in this app -- confirming the start of a new cycle -- and it is treated like an admin task buried in a form.

I want a big, warm, prominent button that appears on my dashboard around the time my period is predicted. Something like "Has your period started?" with a "Yes, today" and "Not yet" option. When I tap "Yes, today," I want the app to acknowledge it -- not just recalculate the phase wheel silently, but say something like "New cycle, Day 1. Your body is doing important work. We are here with you." Make it a moment, not a database update.

And if I tap "Not yet" for 3+ days past the prediction, I want the app to gently say "Your cycle seems longer than usual this month. That is completely normal -- cycles vary. If you are concerned, consider talking to your doctor." Not medical advice, just reassurance. Because when my period is late, I am scared, and the app currently pretends everything is fine.

**3.5 stars.** I will round up to 4 on the App Store because the concept deserves support.

To reach 5 stars:

### Q6: "Did anything about the app make you uncomfortable or feel judged?"

Two things.

First, the conception chance card on my dashboard. "Kha nang thu thai: Rat cao" (Conception Chance: Very High) sitting right there on my main screen. One time Minh glanced at my phone and I instinctively tilted it away because I didn't want him to see "Very High fertility" staring back at us. That felt exposed. The feature is useful when I am checking privately, but it should not be the second-most-prominent card on my dashboard. Make it expandable or move it to a details section.

Second, the worry about data visibility. For the first two weeks, I did not know if Minh could see my mood scores, my symptoms, my conception chance. The app never told me what is shared and what is private. I eventually figured out from his dashboard screenshots that he only sees my phase and a mood description, not my actual numbers. But I should not have to figure that out through detective work. The uncertainty made me self-censor my check-in data for a few days -- I logged mood 3 when it was really mood 1 because I thought he might see it. That defeats the purpose of honest tracking.

I never felt judged by the language or tone, though. The app never said "your hormones are making you act this way" or anything reductive. The phase descriptions are empathetic and affirming. "Your body is working very hard" is the opposite of judgment. That part is excellent.

### Q7: "Is there a moment where the app genuinely helped you? Describe it."

Day 20, the SOS cramps moment.

I woke up with my period and the cramps were brutal. Minh had already left for work. I was on the couch with a heating pad and I needed him to bring home pain meds and ginger tea. I could have called, but I was in that state where talking felt like too much effort. I opened the app, went to SOS, tapped "Cramps Alert." Done.

That was the moment the app justified its existence. Not just for the convenience -- I could have texted -- but for the emotional weight. Sending an SOS signal feels different from typing "can you bring ibuprofen." The SOS says "I am in pain and I need you" without me having to perform vulnerability over text. And on Minh's end, receiving an SOS signal with a red card and specific action items is different from a text saying "my stomach hurts." The urgency was communicated instantly.

I would keep the app on my phone for this feature alone.

### Q8: "How does this compare to Flo/Clue/other period apps you've tried?"

I used Flo for about 6 months and Clue for maybe 3. Here is my honest comparison:

**Where Easel wins:**
- The couples dimension. Flo and Clue are solo apps. My cycle data just sat there. Easel sends it to someone who can actually act on it. This is the fundamental differentiator.
- The emotional tone. Flo feels like a medical app with pink paint. Clue feels like a science textbook. Easel feels like a warm friend who happens to know about cycles.
- The Moon/Sun metaphor. "You are the Moon" is beautiful. "You are User #47291 tracking your menstrual cycle" is what Flo feels like.
- The Vietnamese quality. Flo's Vietnamese is Google Translate level. Easel's reads like a native speaker wrote it.
- The Whisper and SOS features. No other app has anything like this.

**Where Flo/Clue still win:**
- Flo has 50+ symptom options. Easel has 8. Sometimes I want to log "acne" or "insomnia" and there is no option.
- Clue has cycle analytics -- charts showing mood trends over multiple cycles, average cycle length tracking, prediction accuracy metrics. Easel has none of this.
- Both have community features (forums, articles). Easel has no educational content beyond the phase descriptions.
- Both remind me to log. Easel does not.
- Both let me log period start from the main screen. Easel buries it in Settings.

**The bottom line:** Easel is not trying to be Flo or Clue. It is doing something they cannot do -- making my cycle useful for my relationship, not just for me. But the basic tracking features need to catch up. I should not have to choose between "the app my boyfriend uses" and "the app that actually tracks well."

### Q9: "Do you trust this app with your health data? Why or why not?"

Mostly yes, with one reservation.

The health sync education screen during onboarding was outstanding. Three bullet points: "We only read menstrual cycle data," "Your data stays on your device," "You can disconnect anytime." That was exactly what I needed to hear. The shield icon, the "Your data, your choice" tagline -- it felt genuine, not like corporate privacy-washing.

I chose manual entry instead of HealthKit sync on first use, but I am considering enabling Health sync now that I trust the app. The fact that the manual option was presented without guilt or pressure was a big trust signal.

My reservation is about couple data sharing. The app never explicitly told me what Minh can and cannot see. I eventually figured it out, but during the first two weeks I was operating on assumption. For a couples app handling health data, the trust boundary between "my data" and "our data" needs to be crystal clear. It is not.

Also, the app stores data in Supabase (cloud), not purely on-device. The education screen says "Your data stays on your device" which is technically about HealthKit read access, but it creates an impression that ALL my data is local. My daily mood logs, symptoms, and period dates are in a database. That is fine and necessary for the couples feature to work, but the privacy messaging should be more precise about what stays local versus what goes to the server.

### Q10: "If Minh stopped using the app, would you keep using it alone?"

Probably not for more than a month.

The core value of Easel for me is the bridge to Minh. The Whisper feature, the SOS alerts, the knowledge that he is getting phase-aware advice -- that is why I use it. Without Minh on the other end, Easel becomes a bare-bones period tracker with 8 symptoms, no analytics, no cycle history visualization, and no reminder notifications. At that point, Flo does the solo tracking job better.

I would miss the Phase Wheel -- it is beautiful and I have an emotional attachment to seeing my day number in that glowing circle. I would miss the Vietnamese greetings that feel like they were written for me. But those are aesthetic pleasures, not functional value.

If Minh stopped using the app, I would give it one more cycle to see if I valued the solo experience. If the AI insights stayed repetitive and the tracking features stayed basic, I would switch back to Flo and keep Easel installed but dormant.

The honest answer is: Easel is a couples app. Without the couple, it is an art piece with insufficient utility.

## Part II -- Minh (Sun) Exit Interview

Yes. I hate admitting it because I thought this would be a gimmick, but yes.

Before this app, I had no framework for understanding why Linh's mood shifted. She would be energetic and fun on Monday and withdrawn and snappy by Friday, and I had no idea why. That knowledge alone -- just knowing there is a biological rhythm -- changed how I interpret her behavior.

But the app did not just educate me. It told me what to *do*. The advice card that said "Stock up on her favorite snacks and avoid arguments" on Day 26 of her cycle -- that directly led to me buying her banh trang tron. She was shocked that I thought of it. I didn't tell her the app suggested it. I just looked like a thoughtful boyfriend.

So did it help me be a better boyfriend? Yes -- not by changing who I am, but by giving me information I never had access to before. It is like having a cheat code for empathy.

Where it failed: the Day 18 argument. The app had told me at lunch to "be patient and avoid arguments." I forgot by dinner. The advice was right but the delivery was wrong -- a 6 PM push notification saying "Be extra gentle tonight" would have saved us both an hour of cold silence. The app is a pull experience for me. I need it to push.

### Q2: "Was there a moment where you were glad you had the app?"

Day 20. The SOS cramps alert.

### Q3: "What did the app ask of you that felt like too much effort?"

So nothing felt like too much effort because nothing was required. But ironically, the lack of any interactive element made me forget the app existed. On days when no notification came and no signal arrived, I had zero reason to open it. The app treated me like a passive reader of a blog, not a participant in a relationship tool.

If you are asking what I would *not* want to do: anything that takes more than 30 seconds. No journaling, no long surveys, no sharing my feelings in a text box. But a quick "Did you check in on Moon today?" toggle would be fine. One tap. That is my ceiling.

### Q5: "Rate the app: 1-5 stars. What would make it a 5 for you?"

**3 stars.** The concept is 5 stars. The execution has gaps.

To make it a 5:
1. **Daily push notification at 6 PM** with phase-aware advice. "Moon is in Luteal Day 25. Be patient tonight. Consider picking up her favorite snack on your way home." This one feature would change everything. I would open the app every day.

3. **A real calendar.** Show me when her period is predicted, when the good-energy phases are, which weekends to plan dates and which to stay home. I should not have to do mental math with the countdown badge.

Fix those five and I am a loyal daily user giving you 5 stars and telling all my friends.

### Q6: "Would you keep using this in 3 months? Why or why not?"

If nothing changes from the current version? Honestly, probably not.

Here is why: my engagement pattern over 30 days was notification-dependent. I opened the app 14 times in 30 days. Of those, 4 were triggered by push notifications and 2 were prompted by Linh showing me her phone. Only 6 were voluntary. That means 57% of my engagement was externally triggered, not habit-driven.

Without a daily push notification, I will gradually forget the app during the long stretches between notifications. There are only 3 notifications per cycle (period approaching, period started, period ended). That is 3 touchpoints in 28 days. On the other 25 days, I have to remember to open the app myself. I will not.

If the daily push notification gets added? Yes, absolutely. That one feature transforms my experience from "occasionally useful reference" to "daily relationship companion." I would keep it for years.

The ultimatum is simple: **if the app stops sending me notifications, I delete it.** The notifications are the product for me. The dashboard is supplementary.

The Moon experience is thoughtful, emotional, detailed -- check-ins, insights, whispers, SOS, a beautiful phase wheel. The Sun experience is a read-only dashboard with no interactive elements, an empty calendar tab, and 3 notifications per month. You gave Moon a garden and gave Sun a window to look at it through.

No engagement hooks. No way for me to respond to a whisper with more than "Got it." No way to plan ahead. No acknowledgment that I am doing my part.

I am not asking for parity. Sun should be simpler than Moon -- I do not want to log symptoms or track a cycle. But I need the app to reach out to me daily, show me a calendar I can plan around, and let me close the loop when Linh signals me.

Build the Sun experience with the same love you built the Moon experience, and you will have an app that actually changes relationships.

## Part V -- Synthesis & Prioritized Recommendations

### What the Debrief Reveals

The exit interviews confirm and deepen findings from all previous phases. Three meta-themes emerge:

**1. The app is a bridge, not two solo tools.**
Both personas define the app's value in relational terms. Linh's most valued feature (Whisper) is about reaching Minh. Minh's most valued feature (notification) is about knowing Linh's state. Neither would use the app alone for long. Product decisions must optimize for the *bridge* -- the quality of signal transmission between Moon and Sun -- not for individual tracking quality.

**2. The notification system is the product for Sun.**
Minh's 14/30 engagement rate, his explicit statement that he would delete the app if notifications stopped, and his primary request for daily push notifications all point to one conclusion: the Sun experience IS the notification experience. The dashboard is supplementary. Building Sun features means building Sun notifications.

**3. Trust is fragile and earned in specifics.**
Linh's trust was nearly broken by two things: the phantom phase wrapping during a late period, and the uncertainty about what Minh can see. Both are information failures -- the app either showed the wrong information or withheld necessary information. Trust recovery requires: period confirmation flow (accurate data), data visibility disclosure (transparent sharing), and whisper delivery confirmation (closed emotional loops).

### Final Verdict

Easel has found product-market fit at the concept level. The Moon/Sun metaphor, the Whisper and SOS signal system, the phase-aware partner advice -- these are genuine innovations in the couples health app space. No competitor offers this combination.

The execution gaps are significant but fixable. The top five issues (period confirmation, Sun notifications, check-in reminders, notification i18n, whisper confirmation) are all solvable within 1-2 development sprints. None require architectural changes.

Both personas are keeping the app. Both are conditionally committed -- Linh needs the tracking to improve, Minh needs the notifications to arrive. The window to convert conditional users into loyal advocates is approximately one more cycle (28 days). After that, unaddressed friction accumulates into churn.

**Bottom line from Linh:** "The app sees me. It just needs to see me more accurately."

**Bottom line from Minh:** "The app knows what to tell me. It just needs to actually tell me."
